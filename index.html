Trash of the Week – Week Nine
A snapshot of my life in trash


Preface ☆ Overview ☆ Gallery ☆ Table ☆ Coda ☆

Preface

I decided to record a week’s worth of my trash in order to remind myself that what I throw away is a reflection of who I am. Every item, even something as small and ordinary as a napkin, can be tied to a specific moment or event that holds memories and meaning. I chose to focus on the items from this particular week because I enjoy the small stories that come with each piece of trash. They are all stories that may seem insignificant at first but can actually say a lot about my daily life. By collecting and reflecting on these discarded items, I realized how often we mindlessly throw things away without thinking about where they came from or what they represent. This process forces us to be more aware of how much we consume, how frequently we waste things, and how our habits impact the environment. Additionally, reflecting on what and how much we throw away can reveal aspects of our identity. Our trash can show our routines, interests, responsibilities, and lifestyle choices. In some ways, it can even categorize us, offering insight into our age, gender, culture, or occupation. Overall, examining trash turns something overlooked into a meaningful reflection of both personal identity and collective responsibility.

If you liked looking at how trash can correlate with something deeper, check out Sarah Newman Vik Muniz and Sto Len.

Overview

Well, considering that my lingustics final cheat sheet (it was allowed!) from last semester is still inside my trash can, it really goes to show: a, I really don’t throw out my trash much and b, that this is probably a testament to my room possibly smelling because I do not change my trash out enough and I am just nose blind to it. But there really wasn’t anything interesting in my trash this week compared to last week. 99% is napkins and food packaging, and that has not changed at all. One habit I notice is that I clean out things that I find useless fast, like when making the table I only had that ticket for a day, and same with the rubber band, the origami box, the sheet music, and the note. It technically was in my room over break so its duration is long in the table, but the moment I got back it was gone.

I divided my trash into three groups:

The Paper Notes


  • First is my linguistics cheat sheet. It is a piece of letter sized paper. For my final, we are allowed to one sheet of paper front and back. I printed it out three hours before the exam from the busch student center. I threw it out because I don’t want to me reminded of how stressful that exam was. But I did well on it so all good.

  • Second is my notebook note. It is one scrap from a bird notepad that my aunt added in a letter to me a long time ago. I went to an AT&T networking event. I hung it on my whiteboard for like a week and thought i’d address it later but after re-looking at it, none of it makes sense like idk why I wrote “speed” on it… I threw it out for that reason.

  • This is my sheet music when I auditioned for symphony band. It is a piece of letter sized paper. I got in, but I realized I couldn’t commit to going to practice for three hours twice a week on cook/doug and I have to leave an hour early to get there anyway because bus ride there is also too long like last semester. I threw it out because I no longer need the sheet music.

    • Random Memories


  • These are the two batteries from my discussion post. My friend couldn’t use my polaroid, opened up the battery compartment and noticed they were degraded, so I threw it out.

  • This is a paper ticket from a roommate finding event that I went with my roommate because we just wanted the free stuff. They gave out tickets for a raffle, and they email you if you win or not so I threw out my ticket because I don’t need it.

    Coda

    Looking back at this trash, nothing is super surprising. The repetition of napkins, paper, and packaging shows how routine-driven my days are and how easily small, forgettable items can pile up. But even the less exciting pieces still have moments of stress, creativity, and everyday decisions attached to them, reminding me that waste doesn’t have to be interesting to be revealing. I wonder if next week anything will change, whether I become more intentional about what I throw out or if my habits stay exactly the same. Thank you for taking the time to root through my trash with me!
